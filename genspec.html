AI Architecture: Why "General" is Not "Specialized"

A Response to the Recent Concerns Regarding ChatGPT and Medical Advice

By Manas Rath Senior Director of AI & Analytics | Ph.D. in AI & Deep Learning

The recent Oxford University research cited in the news highlights a fundamental truth: ChatGPT is a generalist, not a specialist. To understand why it might fail in a clinical setting while a model like Med-PaLM2 or MedGemma succeeds, we must look at how these systems are built.

1. The Generic Model: A "Student of Everything"

Generic LLMs like ChatGPT are trained on a massive, diverse dataset—essentially everything written by humanity on the internet.

  • How it learns: Like a child in our village observing seasons to learn farming, these models find patterns in data.
  • The Flaw: Because it is trained on "everything," it treats a Reddit post and a peer-reviewed medical journal with similar statistical weight unless specifically tuned.
  • The Result: It acts like a "confident student" who might be wrong but sounds very convincing—a phenomenon we call "hallucination".

2. The Specialized Model: The "Diagnostic Partner"

Domain-specific models (like Med-PaLM 2 or MedGemma) undergo a process called Fine-Tuning and Alignment.

  • Focused Training: These models are primarily trained or "refined" using high-quality medical literature, clinical guidelines, and case studies.
  • Expert Benchmark: For example, Med-PaLM 2 was the first to reach "expert" levels on the US Medical Licensing Examination (USMLE).
  • Constraint: Unlike ChatGPT, these models are designed to admit when they don't know an answer rather than guessing.

3. Addressing the "Complaints": Why Context is King

The article notes that ChatGPT gives inconsistent advice if the prompt changes slightly. This happens because generic AI lacks Clinical Nuance.

  • Data vs. Diagnosis: A doctor collects patient history, lifestyle data, and physical symptoms before deciding. A generic AI only sees the text you type.
  • The "Mixed Bag" Problem: The research shows AI often mixes good advice with bad advice, making it difficult for a layperson to separate the two.
  • Lack of Responsibility: AI has no "moral judgment" or "lived experience"; it calculates probabilities but does not feel the weight of a life-and-death decision.

4. The Human + AI Partnership (The Copilot Approach)

We must shift the conversation from "AI vs. Humans" to "AI + Humans".

  • For Doctors: AI should be a "tireless, encyclopedic second opinion" that flags drug interactions or spots patterns in X-rays that the human eye might miss.
  • For Governance (IAS/OAS): AI can process census and welfare data at scale to help officers make informed decisions, but the final ethical judgment remains human.
  • For the Public: Tools like the Bhashini project or Kisan AI are bringing this intelligence to rural Odisha in the Odia language, but they must be used as guides, not absolute authorities.

5. Myths vs. Reality for Our Community

MYTH REALITY
AI will replace the doctor or the officer. AI amplifies human expertise; it handles routine tasks so experts can focus on complex cases.
AI is always correct. AI can be a "confident student" who is occasionally wrong; human verification is the final word.
AI is a "magic" brain. It is a human-made tool. Like a hammer, it can build or break; our wisdom decides the impact.

Conclusion: Our Responsibility

For a community like Biranarasinghpur—which has always led in knowledge and leadership—the call is not to fear AI, but to understand and guide it. We must ensure that the benefits of specialized AI reach the village clinic and the farmer’s field, not just the glass towers of big cities.

"The machines are learning. The only question is—are we learning too?"
